The Motivation - Why Medicine?

  • Motivation
  • University

Medicine is an incredibly hard career. It is stressful, intense, and emotionally draining, to name but a few of its challenges. Applying for medical school is by no means easy either. Compared to many other ‘good’ degrees, it requires a lot of hard work to both achieve the grades and do all the extra that medical schools require. Being aware of this is key to keeping up the motivation required to get a place but also being prepared for the job that you are signing up for. So, as a first step, I think it’s really important that you discover whether medicine is right for you or not. In this article, I’ll reference what I went through personally to make this decision as well as other thoughts on the process and potential insights I have looking back.

Why medicine?

This is a great question for really assessing your motivation to pursue this career. Is it your parents pressuring you into it, is it because you’ve watched Grey’s Anatomy and thought that it looks like a fun, is it just a default for an academically smart 17-year-old who enjoys the sciences? If any of these are the primary reason you want to do medicine, I seriously suggest you reconsider. I’ve heard from speaking to doctors that there are far easier ways to make money if this also provides you with motivation. In my prep, I repeatedly heard that medicine is a slog, more of an endurance event than a sprint. It requires at least five years at university, two years of foundation training, and then at least three of specialty training. By the time you become a consultant, you will likely be in your 30s, assuming everything goes to plan. Hence, the motivation to pursue it has to be airtight, and it won’t dwindle at the first hurdle you come across. There will be many. By first answering this question, it can also serve as motivation when you are feeling a bit down in the prep or study. Seeing the end goal and what you are aiming for, I found to be really useful. (It’s also a fairly common interview question, so why not get some extra practice in!)

Despite what I’ve said earlier, if you are interested in medicine because of your parents or TV, that’s not a problem, BUT I couldn’t recommend getting work experience enough to help make your decision. Reading about the challenges is one thing, but seeing the tired doctors coming to the end of a night shift really hammers home what you read so often: ‘Medicine is hard’. This is exactly the reason that I sought out work experience, whilst being really useful for talking about in an interview or on your personal statement, the insight that it gave me as to what the career entails was the most important thing that I took away. Online work experience does exist, but I found that it doesn’t get this across anywhere nearly as effectively.

Personally, I chose medicine because of how diverse it is. It combined all of the elements that I valued as important in a career. The practical application of science really appealed to my analytical side, with opportunities for research further down the line. The people-focused nature of it really excited me; seeing how the doctors got to meet such a range of patients and build a relationship with them on work experience appealed to my social side. Personal experience played a role too, seeing how family members suffered from illness, and whilst I couldn’t do much to help, doing what I could was incredibly rewarding. There were lots more reasons that played into my decision as well as many that did their best to put me off.

Ultimately, it’s an incredibly personal decision, and the reasons that it captured my heart may be completely different to yours, and that’s what’s so amazing about this diverse career. If everyone went in for the same reasons and had the same goals, then I don’t know what would happen, but I’m sure it wouldn’t be good. Diversity is so important, and I think this extends to people’s goals and objectives.

There are a lot of warnings out there, including mine about going into this career, and it may seem daunting (it certainly did to me). They’re all created with the best intentions in mind, just ensuring that you know what you’re signing up for, but it certainly might not come across that way! This is just one stage on a long journey, but I want to wish you GOOD LUCK, not that you’ll need it!!

"Luck favours the prepared mind." - Louis Pasteur (1822-1895)
